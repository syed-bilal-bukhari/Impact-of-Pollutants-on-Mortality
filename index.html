Abstract

Air pollution is widely recognized as one of the most critical environmental and public health threats of the 21st century, contributing significantly to global mortality. This research examines the relationship between air pollution exposure and mortality trends on a global scale by integrating three robust, open-access datasets including the State of Global Air, the IHME Death Data with Causes, and the World Bank Population Dataset. These datasets were selected from an initial pool of seven based on their global coverage, temporal depth, data quality, and relevance to public health outcomes.

Using advanced statistical methods and machine learning techniques, this research explores temporal patterns, regional disparities, and population-level vulnerabilities associated with major pollutants, particularly fine particulate matter (PM₂.₅), ozone (O₃), household air pollutant (HAP) and nitrogen dioxide (NO₂). Incorporating population data enables the calculation of per-capita mortality rates, ensuring valid cross-country comparisons and preventing biases driven by differences in national population sizes.

The findings offer critical insights for policymakers, public health authorities, and researchers by identifying high-risk populations and geographic hotspots, and reinforcing the urgency of targeted interventions and stricter air quality standards. Overall, this study demonstrates the potential of data science to address complex, interdisciplinary challenges in environmental epidemiology and global health.

Introduction

Air pollution is one of the most concerning environmental health challenges of our time. The World Health Organization defines it as the presence of harmful substances in the atmosphere, including fine particulate matter (PM₂.₅ and PM₁₀), nitrogen dioxide (NO₂), ozone (O₃), sulphur dioxide (SO₂), and carbon monoxide (CO). Among these, fine particulate matter (PM₂.₅) is particularly harmful because it can penetrate deep into the lungs, enter the bloodstream, and contribute to diseases such as cardiovascular illness, chronic respiratory disease, and premature death. Global assessments show that air pollution is one of the top risk factors for early mortality, responsible for millions of deaths each year (Cohen, et al., 2017) (Di, et al., 2017).

1.1   Background and Motivation

Over the past three decades, progress has been made in monitoring and reducing air pollution. However, the global burden of disease remains high. Estimates suggest that in 2015 alone, exposure to PM₂.₅ caused about 4.2 million deaths worldwide, while ozone exposure caused an additional 254,000 deaths, making air pollution one of the leading global health risks (Cohen, et al., 2017). Importantly, evidence shows that health effects can occur even when air pollution levels are below the legal limits set by international agencies such as the World Health Organization (WHO) and the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) (Di, et al., 2017).

This makes continuous monitoring and deeper understanding of the risks essential. The motivation for this research comes from recognising that air pollution is not only a local or regional problem, but a global one. With the increasing availability of large, high-quality datasets, we can now study mortality patterns across countries and decades, linking pollution exposure to health outcomes in new ways. Using data science techniques to uncover these patterns offers a powerful opportunity to better understand risks, identify long-term trends, and generate evidence that can support public health decisions worldwide.

1.2   Problem Statement

Despite strong evidence linking air pollution with mortality, most existing studies have key limitations. Many focus on specific regions or short time periods, which restricts understanding of long-term global trends. Few studies integrate pollution, cause-of-death, and population datasets to assess how mortality varies over time when adjusted for population size.

Another major concern is that health risks are observed even when pollution levels are below the safety thresholds set by international agencies such as the World Health Organization (WHO) and the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) (Cohen, et al., 2017) (Di, et al., 2017). This raises doubts about whether current air quality standards are sufficient to protect public health.

The problem this analysis addresses is the lack of a comprehensive, global, population-adjusted analysis of how major pollutants (PM₂.₅, NO₂, household air pollution, and O₃) contribute to cause-specific mortality from 1990 to 2020.

1.3   Objectives

The main objective of this analysis is to evaluate how long-term exposure to air pollution contributes to mortality across countries, population sizes, and time periods.

To achieve this, the study pursues the following specific objectives:

  1. To quantify the relationship between exposure to key pollutants (PM₂.₅, O₃, HAP and NO₂) and all-cause and cause-specific mortality using globally corresponding datasets, while adjusting mortality rates relative to national population sizes.
  2. To assess whether pollution exposure remains a significant mortality risk even at concentrations below current international safety thresholds, such as those set by the WHO and U.S EPA.
  3. To identify and compare global, regional, and national temporal trends in both pollution levels and population-adjusted mortality, evaluating changes over time from 1990 to 2020.
  4. To visualize key findings using advanced data visualisation techniques such as time-series plots, heat maps, choropleth maps, and scatter plots highlight geographic hotspots and temporal variations.
  5. To apply both traditional statistical tools (e.g., Pearson correlation, linear and multiple regression) and data-driven modelling approaches (e.g., stratified analysis, predictive modelling, composite pollution index modelling) to provide a multidimensional view of the problem.
  6. To contribute to global environmental health by identifying at-risk populations and highlighting areas where stronger regulations or targeted interventions could deliver the greatest health benefits.

1.4   Research Questions

  1. What are the long-term global and regional trends in air pollution levels, particularly PM₂.₅ and ozone, over the past three decades?
  2. How does long-term exposure to air pollution correlate with population-adjusted, cause-specific mortality, including deaths from cardiovascular, respiratory, and cancer-related diseases?
  3. Which countries and regions show the highest mortality burden from air pollution once adjusted for population size?
  4. Which air pollutants and related factors are the strongest predictors of increased mortality when analysed using data science methods such as correlation analysis, regression models, and clustering?

1.5   Impact and Significance

The findings of this research have both scientific and practical importance. Scientifically, the study contributes to a growing body of evidence on how long-term exposure to air pollution affects health worldwide. By applying advanced statistical and machine learning techniques to global datasets, this analysis shows the value of integrating data science with public health research.

From a policy perspective, the results can help international agencies, governments, and local authorities in setting or revising air quality standards. If the analysis shows that health risks persist below current thresholds, that calls for stricter regulations. If pollutants are identified as hotspots, it can help target interventions where they are most needed.

More broadly, this research highlights the human and societal costs of air pollution, framing it not only as an environmental issue but also as a matter of global health equity. By adjusting mortality rates for population size and tracking changes over time, this research provides a fairer and more comprehensive view of how air pollution affects global mortality.

Dataset Gathering & Selection

2.1   Initial Exploration

A crucial step in this research was identifying datasets capable of supporting a strong investigation into the relationship between air pollution and mortality. The goal was to find sources offering global coverage over an extended time frame, with well-documented and clean variables, and a clear link between pollution indicators and mortality outcomes.

The dataset search was conducted through academic databases (Google Scholar, ScienceDirect, Kaggle, GitHub), institutional repositories such as the World Health Organization (WHO), and open-access environmental data portals. More than a dozen datasets were identified, each differing in geographic coverage, temporal depth, and thematic relevance. After an initial check for completeness, ease of access, and relevance to the research goals, seven datasets were selected for closer evaluation.

2.2   Dataset Selection

Following the exclusion process, three datasets emerged as the most suitable for meeting the research objectives: the State of Global Air Dataset and the IHME Death Data with Causes (Global Burden of Disease). Their strengths lie in their methodological robustness, breadth of coverage, and direct relevance to studying the air pollution-mortality relationship. A third dataset, the World Bank Population Dataset was included to ensure comparability of results across countries of different sizes.

2.2.1   State of Global Air Dataset

  • Source: Health Effects Institute.
  • Content: Provides global, annual, population-weighted data on pollutant levels including PM₂.₅, household air pollution (HAP), NO₂, and ambient ozone for more than 200 countries from 1990 through 2021 (Health Effects Institute, 2024).
  • Justification: This dataset gives the exposure side of the analysis. It offers long-term, reliable measures of major pollutants, which are essential for identifying trends and making fair comparisons across countries and regions.

  • Source: Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation.
  • Content: This dataset provides yearly cause-specific mortality data for 206 countries, covering the period from 1990 to 2023 (with historical estimates available back to the 1960s). It includes more than 300 diseases, conditions, and injuries as causes of death (Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation, 2024).
  • Justification: This dataset was selected because it offers detailed information on deaths by specific causes across more than 200 countries for over three decades. When combined with the State of Global Air dataset, it allows the study to connect pollution exposure with health outcomes in a clear and reliable way.

  • Source: The population dataset was obtained from the datasets/population repository on GitHub (DataHub.io, 2025). Unlike platforms such as Kaggle, where individuals can freely upload datasets, this repository is maintained by Datahub.io, a well-regarded open data provider that aggregates and standardizes datasets from official sources. The dataset is not user-generated but is compiled directly from the official World Bank data (World Bank, 2023), as confirmed in the repository's documentation. The repository has strong community recognition (over 100 stars on GitHub), indicating trust and active use.
  • Data Origins: The World Bank dataset itself compiles data from several authoritative international and national demographic sources, including:
    1. United Nations Population Division (World Population Prospects)
    2. United Nations Statistical Division (Population and Vital Statistics Reports, various years)
    3. Census reports and other publications from national statistical offices
    4. Eurostat (Demographic Statistics)
    5. Secretariat of the Pacific Community (Statistics and Demography Programme)
    6. U.S. Census Bureau (International Database)

By adjusting mortality figures relative to population size (e.g., Crude Death Rate per 1000 people or Population Growth Rate), the analysis ensures fair comparisons across countries and years. This reduces bias and prevents countries being misclassified as "outliers" simply due to population scale rather than true underlying risk. Using per-capita measures therefore provides results that are more accurate, balanced, and meaningful for cross-country and temporal analysis.

Data Preprocessing

3.1   Overview

Before conducting statistical modelling and analysis, it was essential to prepare the raw datasets to ensure reliability, consistency, and compatibility with each other. The data originated from different sources and therefore varied in format and structure. To achieve comparability, a structured preprocessing was implemented. This included selection of analytically relevant features, standardisation of variable names and data types, handling of missing values, detection and handling of outliers and alignment of country-year identifiers. Each dataset was first cleaned individually and subsequently integrated into a unified master dataset covering 1990–2020.

3.2   Pollutants Data

The pollutants dataset was compiled from four CSV files containing annual country-level exposure estimates for Household Air Pollution (HAP), Nitrogen Dioxide (NO₂), Ozone (O₃), and Particulate Matter (PM₂.₅). Raw files contained Excel-style artefacts (e.g., values stored as "=Country"), which were corrected using string replacement and type conversion to ensure numerical compatibility.

Feature selection focused on retaining only essential attributes. Redundant identifiers were excluded as they duplicated existing information or provided no analytical value. A detailed list of dropped features is:

3.2.2   Outlier Detection

Outlier detection for environmental time series data requires special consideration, since pollution levels naturally change over time due to industrialization, policy changes, and other factors. Using traditional methods such as IQR across the entire dataset can incorrectly flag outliers, simply because they represent a different time period.

Given that our dataset has yearly granularity, with only one observation per country per year, detection approaches need to account for this structure. We implemented and compared the following methods:

  • Year-Specific Comparison: For each year, pollution levels are compared across all countries, identifying countries with unusually high or low values relative to global patterns for that specific year.
  • Country-Specific Temporal Deviation: Each country's historical pattern is analysed to identify years that deviate significantly from the country's expected temporal trend.

These approaches account for both the yearly granularity of the data and the importance of considering temporal trends when identifying genuine outliers in environmental datasets. Since the dataset was gathered from reputable sources, it was expected that there would be no true outliers in the data.

3.2.3   Resultant Dataset

We merged the 4 files into 1 master pollutant dataset and to avoid ambiguity when merging pollutants, variable names were standardised with pollutant-specific suffixes (e.g., Exposure Mean PM₂.₅, Exposure Mean NO₂). The four datasets were merged on Country and Year, producing a consolidated panel of multi-pollutant exposures.

Feature Engineering

In this section, we develop a set of quantitative measures to evaluate global air pollution exposure and its human impact. These formulas capture different dimensions of the problem: from population-weighted exposure levels and indexed trends to combined pollution indices, total human impact, and weighted correlations between pollutants. Together, they provide a consistent framework for comparing exposures across countries, pollutants, and years, while ensuring that results reflect real population sizes and global health relevance.

4.1   Global Population-Weighted Exposure

Definition

A measure of the average pollutant exposure for the global population in a given year, weighting each country's exposure by its population size.

Formula
$$\text{Exposure}_{\text{PW}} = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{c} \text{Exposure}_{c} \times \text{Population}_{c}}{\displaystyle\sum_{c} \text{Population}_{c}}$$

Purpose

Ensures global averages reflect population size. Larger populations influence the global mean more than smaller ones, giving a realistic measure of worldwide exposure.

4.2   Combined Pollution Index (CPI)

Definition

The CPI aggregates multiple pollutants into a single score, making them comparable by normalization. It reflects the overall pollution burden across pollutants.

Formula
Step 1: Min-max normalization of each pollutant to [0, 1]
$$X_{\text{norm}}(c, y, p) = \frac{X(c, y, p) - \min_{c', y'} X(c', y', p)}{\max_{c', y'} X(c', y', p) - \min_{c', y'} X(c', y', p)}$$
Step 2: Summation across all pollutants, giving CPI in range [0, 4]
$$\text{CPI}(c, y) = \sum_{p \in P} X_{\text{norm}}(c, y, p)$$

Purpose

Provides a dimensionless, comparable score across pollutants. Countries with high exposure to multiple pollutants score higher, highlighting the overall pollution burden instead of focusing on one pollutant.

4.3   Pollution Burden (Total Human Impact)

Definition

Total Human Impact measures the overall pollution burden on a country's population. It combines pollution intensity (CPI) with population size, showing both how polluted a country is and how many people are affected.

Formula
Step 1: Multiply CPI with population size
$$\text{TotalPollutionBurden}_{c,y} = \text{CPI}_{c,y} \times \text{Population}_{c,y}$$
Step 2: Log transformation to reduce skew from large populations
$$L_{c,y} = \log\!\left(1 + \text{TotalPollutionBurden}_{c,y}\right)$$
Step 3: Min-max scaling to [0, 100]
$$\text{ScaledPollutionBurden}_{c,y} = \frac{L_{c,y} - \min_{c',y'}\!(L_{c',y'})}{\max_{c',y'}\!(L_{c',y'}) - \min_{c',y'}\!(L_{c',y'})} \times 100$$

Purpose

Captures the true human burden of pollution by including both intensity and population. Log-scaling reduces skew from very large countries (e.g., China, India), and normalization makes results comparable across countries and years.

  • 100: country with maximum global impact (after log-scaling)
  • 0: country with minimum global impact
  • 50: median global impact after scaling
  • Log-scaling reduces extreme outliers while keeping comparisons meaningful.

4.4   Weighted Pearson Correlation

Definition

The weighted Pearson correlation measures the linear relationship between two variables when each observation has a weight. Here, each country-year is weighted by its population, so the correlation reflects the relationship as experienced by the average person, not the average country.

  • \(w_i\): weight (population of country \(i\))
  • \(x_i, y_i\): exposure values for two pollutants
  • \(\mu_x, \mu_y\): weighted means of \(x\) and \(y\)
Step 1: Weighted Mean
$$\mu_x = \frac{\sum_i w_i \, x_i}{\sum_i w_i} \qquad \mu_y = \frac{\sum_i w_i \, y_i}{\sum_i w_i}$$

Represents the average value, adjusted for population size.

Step 2: Weighted Variance & Covariance
$$\text{var}_w(x) = \frac{\sum_i w_i\,(x_i - \mu_x)^2}{\sum_i w_i}$$ $$\text{cov}_w(x, y) = \frac{\sum_i w_i\,(x_i - \mu_x)(y_i - \mu_y)}{\sum_i w_i}$$
Step 3: Weighted Pearson Correlation
$$\rho_w = \frac{\text{cov}_w(x, y)}{\sqrt{\text{var}_w(x) \cdot \text{var}_w(y)}}$$
Fully Substituted Formula
$$\rho_w = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_i w_i\,(x_i - \mu_x)(y_i - \mu_y)}{\sqrt{\displaystyle\sum_i w_i\,(x_i - \mu_x)^2} \;\cdot\; \sqrt{\displaystyle\sum_i w_i\,(y_i - \mu_y)^2}}$$

Reduces to standard Pearson correlation if all \(w_i\) are equal.

Purpose

Weighted correlation ensures larger populations influence results more than small ones, making it representative of real human exposure.

  • If unweighted: each country contributes equally, regardless of population size.
  • If weighted: larger countries contribute more, reflecting global experience more accurately.

Analysis

As the world has industrialized and urbanized over the past three decades, a fundamental question to assess is: Are we winning or losing the battle against air pollution? While headlines often focus on individual cities or dramatic events, the global picture requires a data-driven lens to understand the complex relationship between pollution exposure, population dynamics and human health outcomes. This analysis employs population weighted metrics rather than simple country averages which is a crucial methodological choice that ensures our insights reflect human impact, not just geography. When we say "global air quality is improving" we mean the average person's exposure is declining, not just that more countries happen to have better air. The answers we uncover will inform billions of dollars in public health investments and policy decisions affecting billions of lives. By the end of this analysis, we'll know not just whether air quality is improving globally but where progress is happening, what drives it and how it translates into human health outcomes.

5.1   Pollutants

5.1.1   Global Pollutants at a Glance

To start with, we can see through three decades of global air quality data and answer that after three decades of environmental policies, economic development and technological innovation, is the world's air getting cleaner or dirtier?

Upon analysing, we figured out that it's not straightforward because different pollutants tell different stories. Success with one pollutant doesn't guarantee success with others. We computed a population weighted global average index of pollutant while considering 1990 as baseline and concluded that three pollutants improved dramatically while Ozone worsens, revealing the chemical complexity of air quality management.

From the table below, we can see that HAP represents public health's greatest victories with a 36% decline showing that hundreds of millions gained access to clean cooking fuels. It can be driven by rural electrification, LPG subsidies such as India's Ujjwala Yojana (PMUY & Wikipedia, 2016) and other economic developments. The deadliest air pollutant PM₂.₅ also declined 21% which reflects successful implementation of air quality standards, industrial controls, and better urban planning. Another important pollutant majorly caused by transportation and vehicle emissions is NO₂ which declined about 18% which can be based on initiatives like strict Euro standards and Clean Air Act (Castellanos & Boersma, 2012) (Krotkov, et al., 2017). Another factor can be increase in the use of electric vehicles (Chang, Huang, & Chaveste, 2023).

We can see difference in trends for different pollutants from the above figures. The global PM₂.₅ shows a slight increase in early 2000s before a sharp drop after 2013 (shown in Figure 1). The initial bump corresponds to surging industrial growth and pollution in rapidly developing nations (particularly in Asia), followed by improvements as pollution control measures took effect. Seeing Figure 2, we can see that all top 10 populous countries showed PM₂.₅ reductions from 1990 to 2020. Notably, China's aggressive air quality policies in the 2010s including the "Air Pollution Prevention and Control Action Plan" of 2013 likely contributed to the post-2013 global PM₂.₅ decline. Based on our data, China's average PM₂.₅ exposure fell from 49.3 to 34.8 μg/m³ resulting in a 29.4% decrease. While all countries showed PM₂.₅ reductions from 1990 to 2020, it's important to note that current levels in many countries still vastly exceed the WHO guideline of 5 μg/m³.

Ozone

The global Ozone trend reveals a different pattern compared to other pollutants. Unlike others which showed significant improvements, Ozone levels have remained relatively upward. Ozone is a secondary pollutant formed through complex photochemical reactions involving precursor emissions (NOₓ and VOCs) and influenced by temperature and sunlight intensity. Countries in Asia like Bangladesh, India, Indonesia, Pakistan & China notably contributed in ozone. The cause can be rising temperature due to climate change or increased urbanization and motorization in developing countries leading to higher NOₓ and VOC emissions.

Nitrogen Dioxide

Unlike PM₂.₅ which showed moderate overall decline, and unlike ozone which showed consistent increases, NO₂ demonstrates a more complex pattern with a relatively stable period in the 1990s, a notable peak around 2005–2010 and a significant decline after 2010. The peak period corresponds to rapid industrialization in developing countries, particularly in Asia while the post-2010 decline likely reflects the impact of:

  1. Stricter vehicle emission standards (Euro 6, Tier 3, etc.)
  2. Increased adoption of catalytic converters and NOₓ reduction technologies
  3. Shifts toward cleaner fuels and renewable energy in power generation
  4. Targeted urban air quality management in major cities

We see Bangladesh, India & Indonesia having substantial deterioration in NO₂ levels which highlights how economic development pathways and regulatory approaches strongly influence air quality outcomes, with NO₂ reductions being most challenging in rapidly urbanizing regions and increasing vehicle fleets.

Household Air Pollution

The global household air pollution trend reveals a steady decline over the past three decades, indicating substantial progress in reducing indoor air pollution worldwide with Brazil having the largest percentage reduction. This decreasing trend is particularly significant in high income countries through access to clean cooking and electricity thus, HAP primarily affects rural and low-income populations.

As far as policy making is concerned, the focus should be in the red quadrant which requires high-priority intervention but to ensure that policy interventions are effective, it is essential to first identify the root causes of air quality deterioration. However, the source of pollutants often varies significantly by region. For example, in South Asia, particularly countries like India, air pollution is heavily linked to older vehicles on the road, weak emissions standards, and slower adoption of electric vehicles. Meanwhile, in parts of Sub-Saharan Africa, especially in rapidly industrializing but under-regulated areas, heavy industrial activity and reliance on low-quality fuels are the primary contributors. Since each of these contexts requires a distinct policy response, it becomes critical to analyse pollutants at a more localized, regional level.

As mentioned in research gaps, it's very critical to understand the correlation of pollutants with each other, not just that but on a more granular level to understand the relationship between pollutants on regional scale. This would highlight the sources; the key reasons of pollution emission guiding the policy makers to pinpoint the exact reason and make better policies accordingly. Thus, we calculated cross-pollutant correlation by using our weighted Pearson correlation formula.

We firstly calculated the global correlation represented in Figure 6, and then a per-region cross-pollutant correlation shown in Figure 7.

PM₂.₅ shows very different relationships with other pollutants depending on the region. In Asia (r = 0.73) and Europe (r = 0.48), it has moderate to strong positive correlations with O₃. The reason for this is high O₃ levels can boost the formation of secondary particles under hot conditions and in cities with heavy NOₓ emissions (He, et al. 2024) (Wang, Zhao, Zhang, & Hu, 2023). In contrast, Oceania shows a strong negative correlation (r = −0.90), likely because PM₂.₅ contributes to O₃ loss through chemical reactions on particle surfaces (Qu, 2023). PM₂.₅ also has consistently high correlations with household air pollution (HAP) in most regions (r = 0.56–1.00), since both come from combustion sources. Africa, however, shows weaker correlations, suggesting different emission sources. Policies should therefore be adapted. The regions with high PM₂.₅ and O₃ links, strategies should focus on NOₓ and VOC control, while in Oceania, reducing particle emissions may be more effective.

O₃ and NO₂ correlations reveal differences in chemistry and emissions between regions. In Africa (r = 0.30) and Oceania (r = 0.82), positive correlations suggest NOₓ-limited conditions, where O₃ increases with NO₂. In contrast, Europe (r = −0.13) and the Americas (r = 0.68) show mixed patterns shaped by urban and rural contrasts. Studies also show that PM₂.₅ levels in cities are strongly influenced by NO₂, CO, and SO₂, explaining almost half of the total variation (J., 2024). Household emissions worsen the situation as about 2.1 billion people still rely on kerosene, biomass, and coal for cooking, producing HAP that reduces O₃ formation (Organisation, 2021). This means O₃ control needs to be region-specific. NOₓ reduction is key in VOC-limited urban areas, while VOC reduction is more important in NOₓ-limited rural areas.

NO₂ patterns reflect differences in economic development and energy use. In Africa (r = −0.78) and the Middle East/North Africa (r = −0.67), NO₂ has strong negative correlations with HAP, while Europe shows only a weak negative link (r = −0.27). This suggests that as economies shift from biomass to fossil fuels, NO₂ levels rise even as HAP falls (Hickman, et al., 2021). Africa's unique pattern, where NO₂ has only a weak positive link with O₃ (r = 0.30) but a strong negative link with HAP, highlights the dominance of biomass burning. Biomass combustion releases PM, NOₓ, CO, SO₂, and other hazardous pollutants (Partnership for Policy Integrity, 2021). Correlations between NO₂ and PM₂.₅ also vary, positive in Africa (r = 0.33) and Europe (r = 0.17), but negative in Asia (r = −0.09) and Oceania (r = −0.84). These differences show how local weather and chemistry influence secondary particle formation. Policies should therefore promote clean household energy in Africa and MENA and focus on traffic and industry in more developed regions.

HAP (Household Air Pollution) correlations highlight the role of energy access. Across regions, HAP is strongly correlated with PM₂.₅ (r = 0.56–1.00), confirming that household emissions from solid fuels are a major source of fine particles (Pratiti, 2021). However, its correlations with other pollutants differ. In Africa, NO₂ and HAP correlations are strongly negative (r = −0.78), while in the Americas they are slightly positive (r = −0.30), reflecting different stages of energy transition. Globally, nearly half the population still uses biomass cookstoves, which generate CO, PM₂.₅, and toxic hydrocarbons (Fandiño-Del-Rio, et al., 2020). HAP–O₃ correlations are consistently negative, since household emissions usually occur indoors, at night, or in polluted conditions that prevent O₃ formation. This shows the urgent need for clean cooking programs in Africa and Asia, while high-income regions should target the last remaining solid-fuel users in disadvantaged communities.

Analysis — Mortality

5.2   Mortality

Having explored how pollutant levels such as PM₂.₅ and NO₂ have changed over the past three decades, it is equally important to examine how mortality has evolved in parallel. This allows us to better understand whether reductions in pollutant exposure have translated into health gains or whether other global forces are shaping mortality patterns.

The first step is to look at total deaths from all causes worldwide. As shown in Figure 8, global deaths have steadily increased from around 57 million in 1990 to more than 83 million in 2020. For most of this period, the increase follows a gradual but steady upward trend. This growth is largely explained by population growth and ageing. As the world's population has increased, and as people are living longer, the number of older adults has grown rapidly. Since older age groups have higher risks of death, this naturally leads to more deaths overall, even though death rates within each age group have generally improved (Cheng, et al., 2020) (Li, Zhou, Wang, & al., 2023). What stands out in the data, however, is the sharp spike in 2020. Unlike the gradual increase of earlier years, this sudden jump is linked to the COVID-19 pandemic, which caused millions of excess deaths worldwide and disrupted normal mortality trends (Msemburi, et al., 2023). This shows how global health shocks, such as pandemics, can abruptly reverse long-term progress in mortality.

To understand this shift more clearly, Figure 9 shows the year-on-year change in deaths, both as absolute numbers and percentage change. From 1990 to around 2019, changes are relatively small and stable, with annual increases typically under 2%. This reflects the steady demographic drivers mentioned above. However, in 2020 the trend is broken: total deaths rose by more than six million compared to the previous year, an increase of over 8%. This spike is consistent with global health data showing excess mortality during the pandemic period. These trends show an image of mortality but doesn't really answer a very important question.

5.2.1   Is the world growing or dying overall?

To answer this, we calculated Crude Death Rate (CDR) — the number of deaths per 1,000 population, normalizing deaths relative to population size for fair comparison across countries. We also calculated Population Growth Rate (PGR), the year-over-year percentage growth scaled to per-1,000 units for direct comparability with CDR, and then the Growth–Mortality Gap between them.

A very concerning insight generated from this graph shows that globally the growth rate of population is decreasing at an alarming trend of −0.18 every year, while the death rate is relatively consistent. We can see that from 1990 to 2020, the gap steadily shrinks toward zero, and by the late 2019 the two curves intersected. This marks the point where net growth effectively reached zero. The sharp rise in deaths during 2020 is largely attributable to the pandemic and can be considered an outlier, but even discounting that anomaly, the underlying trend indicates that population stagnation or decline is no longer a distant possibility but an emerging reality.

5.2.1.1   Growth Rates

To see how the growth rates are fluctuating across the globe and which country is affected the most, we calculated the 30-year average of mortality gap between CDR and PGR, and Figure 11 shows the distribution of gap globally lies at 3.70 which is alarming. To see deeper insights into each country level, Figure 12 shows a world map of this 30-year average gap. Looking at the world map, clear regional patterns emerge in how the population growth and death rate has evolved over the past 30 years.

In Africa, most countries are shaded yellows and greens, indicating that population growth continues to outpace death rates. The Middle East shows a similar pattern of resilience. Countries such as Saudi Arabia, Iraq & Jordan are representing green zones. Fertility here has not fallen as sharply as in Europe or East Asia.

However a stark contrast is visible in Europe, which is dominated by deep reds. These shades reveal regions where deaths now exceed births, which shows a long-term demographic decline. Eastern Europe and Russia are particularly striking with the strongest negative gaps linked to aging populations, persistently low fertility rates and elevated mortality compared to Western Europe.

In Asia, the patterns diverge sharply between regions. South Asia including Pakistan, India & Bangladesh still shows positive zones reflecting modest but positive gaps. However the trajectory is clearly downward. East Asia by contrast is marked in reds, reflecting severe demographic challenges faced by China, Japan and South Korea. These countries are already experiencing population shrinkage, driven by a mix of low fertility, rapid aging and rising mortality.

Taken together, the map reveals a world divided. Global North including Europe, East Asia, North America, and Oceania is already at or near demographic decline, while Global South particularly Africa, Middle East & part of South Asia remains in a growth phase.

Understanding global mortality requires more than counting deaths; it is equally important to visualise the underlying causes and how different diseases contribute to the overall burden. Predictive Modelling

Understanding the relationship between pollutant exposure and mortality rates is crucial for public health policy development, environmental regulations and early warning systems. We created this global dataset and explored key patterns and now this analysis employs a comparative machine learning approach evaluating seven different algorithms ranging from linear models to advanced ensemble methods. The goal is to identify the most effective approach for predicting air pollution-related mortality.

6.1   Feature Selection

Predictors

In the last section, we computed that there are 55 causes which strongly correlate with pollutants having r value greater than 0.5 and from those 55 causes we took top 10 causes per pollutant ultimately resulting in 30 unique causes of deaths. With this selection from the complete dataframe, we were left with 12% of rows (~189,720) but it was much more meaningful since all causes are not related to pollutants, adding those causes would add noise to the dataset. For example, including homicide or accident deaths (which aren't caused by pollution) distracts the model from concentrating on pollution-related mortality only.

We dropped columns that won't be used as predictors. We used only the "Mean" exposure values for pollutants and dropped the "Lower"/"Upper" exposure bounds (these are confidence interval limits and are highly correlated with the mean). Keeping only the mean PM₂.₅, NO₂, O₃, and HAP exposures avoids multicollinearity and reduces feature count. Likewise, we dropped "Deaths Lower" and "Deaths Upper" (if those represent uncertainty ranges), since we'll predict the central mortality rate.

Target Variable

We ensured the target Mortality Rate is computed for each record as: Mortality Rate = (Deaths / Population) × 100,000. We used this standardized mortality rate as the outcome so that country size is accounted for.

6.2   Feature Engineering

Log Transformation

We applied a logarithmic transform to the mortality rate to handle right-skewed distribution. Death counts and rates can vary widely between countries (large populations) and causes, and a log transform will stabilize variance and normalize the distribution. This often leads to more accurate and robust models by reducing the influence of extreme values.

Similarly, we applied log1p to highly skewed input features: population size and pollutant exposure levels. Pollution exposure data (especially PM₂.₅ or NO₂ concentrations) and population counts usually have long-tailed distributions. Logging these features makes their distributions more Gaussian and linearizes multiplicative relationships, which helps linear models fit better and can improve tree-based model performance as well. For instance, an increase from 10 to 20 μg/m³ PM₂.₅ might have a bigger health impact than 0 to 10, and using log emphasizes relative changes.

Scaling

After log transformation, the features are more comparable in scale. So, we standardized continuous features especially for linear/ridge/lasso models so that coefficients are on comparable scales. This prevents features with larger numeric ranges from dominating just by scale. Also, since tree-based models are scale-invariant, normalization is less critical for those.

Encoding

We implemented one-hot encoding for categorical features like region name and cause of death since most ML models can understand only numerical variables.

6.3   Model Selection

A range of models was chosen to balance interpretability, flexibility, and predictive power. Linear models (Linear Regression, Ridge, Lasso) provide a baseline and are easy to interpret but may miss complex relationships. XGBoost and CatBoost are advanced tree-based models known for handling non-linearities and categorical data. Random Forest, an ensemble of decision trees, is robust to overfitting and effective for tabular data with mixed feature types.

6.4   Comparisons & Results

The dual-axis plot showing both RMSE and R² for each model provides a comprehensive view of predictive accuracy and error. The results plots show that Random Forest achieved the best performance (lowest RMSE, highest R²) on the test set. This indicates it captured the underlying patterns in the data more effectively than other models.

The Random Forest can be seen to be performing better than the rest of the models because the dataset has nonlinear relationships and Random Forest can model complex, non-linear interactions between pollutants, population, and mortality rates, which linear models cannot. It automatically captures interactions between features (e.g., combined effects of multiple pollutants). Random Forest is less sensitive to outliers and noisy data, which are common in real-world environmental datasets. Unlike boosting models, Random Forest performs well with default settings and is less prone to overfitting. Feature importance scores from Random Forest help identify key drivers of mortality.

In summary, Random Forest's flexibility and robustness make it an excellent choice for this predictive task, outperforming both linear and more complex boosting models in this context.

Conclusion

This comprehensive exploratory analysis reveals a world in environmental and demographic transition, where progress in reducing traditional pollutants exists alongside emerging challenges and shows global inequalities. From 1990 to 2020, we observe a remarkable success story in pollution control where household air pollution decreased by 36%, PM₂.₅ by 21%, and NO₂ by 18% demonstrating that coordinated global efforts can yield significant environmental improvements. However, this progress is overshadowed by the alarming 13.5% increase in ozone levels, signalling a new frontier in air quality management that requires urgent attention.

The geographical distribution of pollution burden reveals a troubling pattern of environmental injustice. While Sub-Saharan African nations like Niger, Mali, and Burkina Faso face the highest pollution intensity per capita, it is the densely populated developing countries India, China, Pakistan, Nigeria, and Bangladesh that bear the greatest absolute human impact when population size is considered. This analysis exposes a critical insight: the countries with the most people exposed to dangerous pollution levels are precisely those undergoing rapid economic development, creating a perfect storm of environmental and public health challenges.

Perhaps most concerning is the demographic backdrop against which these pollution patterns unfold. Global population growth rates are declining at an unprecedented −0.18 per 1,000 annually, projecting a stagnation in world growth rate merely by the late 2020s if current trends continue. This demographic shift, combined with the strong correlations observed between different pollutants (particularly PM₂.₅, NO₂, and HAP), suggests that countries struggling with air quality face compound challenges that require comprehensive, multi-pollutant interventions rather than piecemeal approaches.

The mortality analysis reveals profound regional disparities in the balance between population growth and death rates. While Gulf states demonstrate robust growth-mortality gaps driven by economic prosperity, Eastern European and post-Soviet nations show concerning patterns where mortality increasingly outpaces population growth. These demographic fault lines, intersecting with pollution exposure patterns, create a complex global health landscape where environmental interventions must be carefully calibrated to local demographic and economic realities.

Ultimately, this analysis demonstrates that air pollution and mortality are not merely environmental or health issues — they are fundamental challenges of global equity and sustainable development with almost 36% of deaths strongly influenced by pollutants. The data compels us toward a future where pollution reduction efforts must prioritize population-weighted impact, recognizing that effective global health policy lies not just in cleaning the most polluted places, but in protecting the greatest number of people from preventable environmental harm.
